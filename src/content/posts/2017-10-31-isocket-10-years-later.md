isocket, the advertising technology startup I joined over 5 years ago and was acquired almost 3 years ago, had a profound and very personal impact on my life. The details have become less accurate and less important. I tried writing this the week we were acquired and again a few months ago. I want to finish it today because a new chapter is starting tomorrow.

<!--more-->

In early 2008, I took over the web development of GameZombie.tv, a student-run video game review site, in exchange for some course credits at Indiana University. Like many sites now and then, they ran banner ads in hopes of making a few bucks. Theirs were powered by another Bloomington-based startup called isocket who called ad units "sockets." I was told to paste some code snippets and that's all I really knew or cared about ads and isocket.

That summer, I [attended](https://www.flickr.com/photos/noahwesley/2773360902/in/pool-blogindiana2008/) _blogINDIANA_, my first tech conference. John [presented](https://www.flickr.com/photos/noahwesley/2772470487/in/pool-blogindiana2008/) "How to Make (More) Money From Your Blog" which ended with beta invites to isocket. He was also on a [panel](https://www.flickr.com/photos/noahwesley/2773371494/in/pool-blogindiana2008/) talking about [some wine guy](https://www.garyvaynerchuk.com/). I awkwardly introduced myself to him after his presentation saying how much I enjoyed using [SproutBox](http://www.sproutbox.com/) at GameZombie (oops). I signed up maxbeatty.com for isocket that weekend and later learned I was like id 8 in the database.

Much later, John and I realized our circle of friends overlapped at IU. We went to the same house parties hosted by my now-wife Caitlin because their roommates were somehow friends. He dated a girl from my small high school. How we managed to avoid each other in real life was impressive. Thankfully, we followed each other on Twitter and kept in touch that way.

[iSocket moved to Silicon Valley.](https://techcrunch.com/2009/05/08/so-long-federated-media-and-thanks-for-all-the-fish/) I finished my senior year and moved to Chicago for work. My blog hobbled along selling one $10 ad. In the Spring of 2010, iSocket pivoted to a SaaS model charging $49/mo for their commission free service. I had to ask [their customer BFF](https://twitter.com/hup) to deactivate me.

_I have a faded memory of participating in a user research session with Al and Ryan about a new design for "sockets" (they were still confusing)._

After a year of corporate life, I knew it wasn't for me...

He had filled his last opening hours before I called so he put me in touch with a recruiter. After many phone screens and a [quick trip to San Francisco](https://www.instagram.com/p/B-ez2/), I accepted an offer at [Reputation.com](https://www.reputation.com). Going from a big cubicle farm to an industrial, open office where wearing a polo was overdressed was a welcomed change. My work was valued; I was learning a ton and facing meaningful challenges. Startup life was and is for me.

John was sort of the only person I knew in the Bay Area when I first arrived. He asked if I could help with [the launch of BuyAds.com](https://techcrunch.com/2011/09/28/self-service-ad-platform-isocket-grabs-more-funding-partners-with-google-steals-rubicon-exec/). I worked a few nights and weekends to help get [BuyAds.com](http://web.archive.org/web/20110509070139/http://www.buyads.com/?) ready for launch. John bought my old monitor for their office (which I recognized years later because it _did_ have one dead pixel he was worried about but didn't notice).

In August, I convinced Reputation.com to sponsor a booth at a designer fair at 500 Startups. John was there, too. We had dinner afterwards where he caught me up on how iSocket was doing. The Valley, like IU, is a lot smaller and more connected than you think.

By next Spring, I was ready for something new. I called John again and this time he was ready for me to [interview](https://github.com/maxbeatty/backbone-sortable-table). Accepting his offer wasn't an easy decision. Caitlin would be moving back to California in a few months. This new job would be in terms of "us." Reputation.com offered me a raise to stay while John was asking me to take a pay cut. The lure of an early stage startup in Silicon Valley was too much. I had been following John and isocket for the past 4 years. I wanted to be part of the story.

Luckily, I convinced [a good friend](https://www.linkedin.com/in/nhan-phan-a20a348/) to leave Reputation.com and come with me. [We joined iSocket in very good company](http://blog.isocket.com/2012/10/welcoming-five-new-teammates/) just as [the company raised their Series A](http://blog.isocket.com/2012/10/isocket-raises-8m-series-a-launches-new-buyads-pro-product-for-large-advertisers-new-teammates-and-hiring/). There were as many new things to build as there were old things to fix.

---

The next two and half years were a rollercoaster. The company was just 8 people when I joined. Weekly team lunches were nice and spread out in the main conference room. For the first month or two, I worked mostly from an old couch using my own laptop. The WiFi routinely stopped working in the middle of the afternoon. Most customer issues could be resolved by logging into the production database and flipping one or two bits.

My first project was an entirely new product called BuyAds Pro that was supposed to cater to someone called a "media planner." It was being built with Ruby on Rails which I had to learn on the fly since the other two existing products were written in PHP. Shortly thereafter, Nhan and I convinced everyone we should write the new admin tool with Node.js and communicate to the new Java backend with Thrift. Our tech stack was spiraling out of control. We were using more languages than there were programmers so naturally we started hiring.

Hiring is hard; harder when you're paying below market, and even harder when you're a bunch of 20-somethings who don't know how to interview. Through a lot of trial and error, we finally figured out a process. We hired a lot of great engineers and assembled a really great team. Looking back, that's what I'm proud of most.

July 2012 - start

Oct 2013 - move to SF + new CEO

Feb 2014 - more funding

Nov 2014 - exit

The promise of BuyAds Pro for media planners got us in the door at some big ad agencies.

The company continued to grow. We opened an office in New York City to start going after bigger publishers and advertisers.

There were many late nights.

What did I do while I was there?

- BuyAds Pro
- tech transition to Coffee + Java
- IT

What did I learn? What have I taken with me?

- transitioning customer base up market (mom&pop => trading desk => agency)
- team of people
- managing people
- execution
- financial tradeoffs
- pay cut > stock payout
- lower salary throughout
- long hours, fewer benefits
